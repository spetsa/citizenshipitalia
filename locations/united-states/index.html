Italian Citizenship for Americans:
Your Complete Guide

Everything American applicants need to know about consulate jurisdictions, naturalization records, and the Italian citizenship application process from the United States.

Last updated: April 2026

Written by Citizenship Italia Legal Team · Citizenship Italia Last updated: April 2026

Quick reference for US applicants

  • There are 16 Italian consulates in the United States; your jurisdiction depends on your state of residence
  • US consulates have significant backlogs — typically 5–7 years or more for citizenship appointments
  • Court route (ATQ in Rome) is often faster — 1–3 years from filing to judgment
  • Naturalization records are held by USCIS (recent) and NARA (historical); both are searchable online
  • State vital records (births, marriages, deaths) vary significantly by state — sourcing can be complex
  • The US has extensive Italian immigration history — naturalization events, Minor Issues, and 1948 cases are common

Italian consulates in the United States: Jurisdictions

The United States has 16 Italian consulates general, each covering multiple states. Here are the major ones:

Consulate General in New York

Jurisdiction: Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont

Current Wait Time: 5–7 years for citizenship appointments

The largest and busiest US consulate. Applications are queued for years. This is one of the most significant bottlenecks for Italian citizenship in the US.

Consulate General in Chicago

Jurisdiction: Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, Ohio, South Dakota, Wisconsin

Current Wait Time: 4–6 years

The second-largest consulate covering the Midwest. Application backlogs are substantial but slightly shorter than New York.

Consulate General in Los Angeles

Jurisdiction: Alaska, Arizona, California, Hawaii, Nevada, Utah, Wyoming

Current Wait Time: 5–6 years

Covers the entire West Coast and western states. One of the busier consulates due to large population centers.

Consulate General in San Francisco

Jurisdiction: Northern California, Hawaii (some), Nevada

Current Wait Time: 5–7 years

Secondary West Coast consulate. Shares jurisdiction with Los Angeles but has its own application queue.

Consulate General in Atlanta

Jurisdiction: Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas (eastern portion), Virgin Islands

Current Wait Time: 4–5 years

Covers the Southeast. Relatively manageable wait times compared to northern consulates.

Consulate General in Houston

Jurisdiction: Oklahoma, Texas (western portion), New Mexico

Current Wait Time: 3–5 years

Covers south-central US. One of the shorter wait times due to smaller applicant pool.

Other consulates

Smaller consulates exist in Boston, Washington DC, Miami, New Orleans, Denver, and San Juan (Puerto Rico). Contact your state's consulate or visit the Italian Foreign Ministry website to confirm your exact jurisdiction.

US naturalization records: USCIS and NARA

To prove your ancestor became a US citizen, you will need their naturalization record. The exact naturalization date is critical for determining whether the Italian citizenship chain is intact.

USCIS (US Citizenship and Immigration Services)

Holds records of naturalizations after 1906

  • Service: USCIS Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) office
  • How to request: Submit a written FOIA request with your ancestor's name, approximate naturalization date, and state
  • Cost: Usually free; copies may be charged at standard rates ($1–$5 per document)
  • Processing: 4–8 weeks (can be longer if the request is complex or the office is backlogged)
  • Online search: USCIS has a limited online genealogy database (genealogy.uscis.gov) but it is not comprehensive
  • Alternative: Use third-party genealogy sites like Ancestry.com or FamilySearch, which have digitized many USCIS records

NARA (National Archives and Records Administration)

Holds records of naturalizations before 1906 and some historical records

  • Service: NARA has multiple regional offices and the main archive in Washington DC
  • How to request: Submit a request to your regional NARA office or the main DC location
  • Cost: Research and copying fees typically $10–$50 depending on the request
  • Processing: 4–12 weeks depending on complexity and location of records
  • Online search: NARA.gov has digitized collections and a research tool (but not all records are online)
  • In-person research: You can visit a NARA location to review records in person (often faster)

US state vital records: A complex patchwork

Birth, marriage, and death certificates are maintained by each US state individually. There is no unified system, so sourcing can be complex and time-consuming. Here are some general guidelines:

Typical process by state:

  • Online ordering: Many states now offer online vital records ordering (e.g., VitalRecords.com). This is the fastest and most convenient option.
  • By mail: All states accept mail requests to their vital records office. Include a check, required forms, and proof of identity.
  • In person: Some states allow in-person pickup from county or state offices.
  • Cost: $10–$30 per certified copy, varying by state. Apostille fees are additional ($5–$25).
  • Processing: 1–4 weeks depending on the state and whether you use expedited services.

Key states (examples):

  • New York: Search online at vitalrecords.com or contact the New York State Department of Health Vital Records office
  • California: vitalrecords.cdph.ca.gov offers online ordering
  • Texas: TexasVitalStatistics.com or contact the Texas Department of State Health Services
  • Massachusetts: mass.gov vital records portal
  • Pennsylvania: pa.gov vital records office

For any state, search "[State Name] Vital Records" or "[State Name] Vital Statistics" to find the official office.

Why US applications can be complex

American applicants often face additional complexity for several reasons:

Documentation fragmentation:

  • 50 different state vital records systems with varying formats, availability, and ordering processes
  • 16 consulates across the US with different standards and processing times
  • USCIS and NARA records divided between agencies and sometimes difficult to locate

Immigration history:

  • High rates of naturalization events that can trigger the "Minor Issue" (Law 555/1912)
  • Complex family situations with remarriage, name changes, or adoption
  • 1948 cases more common in older Italian-American families

Consulate backlogs:

  • Significant wait times — often 5–7+ years at major offices like New York and Chicago
  • Some consulates less transparent about processing timelines
  • More likely to request follow-up documents during processing

The Italian court route (ATQ) from the United States

Many American applicants choose to file directly in Italian court (Court of Rome) instead of waiting for a consulate appointment. This route:

  • Takes 1–3 years from filing to judgment (significantly faster than consulate)
  • Requires an Italian attorney (€5,000–€8,000 USD in fees, plus €600 government fee)
  • Does not require you to travel to Italy; your attorney handles the case
  • Results in a court judgment recognizing your citizenship, which you then register with the consulate
  • Can address complications like the Minor Issue or 1948 cases that consulates reject outright

For many US applicants with long consulate waits, the court route is financially and time-wise equivalent or better than waiting 6+ years.

Document sourcing tips for American applicants

  • Order extra copies. Obtain 2–3 certified copies of each vital record to avoid re-ordering delays.
  • Request apostilles immediately. When ordering vital records, request apostille authentication at the same time.
  • Start USCIS FOIA requests early. Naturalization record requests can take 8+ weeks; initiate this immediately when planning your application.
  • Use third-party genealogy databases. Sites like Ancestry.com, FamilySearch, and JewishGen have digitized naturalization records and can accelerate your search.
  • Confirm consulate requirements before translating. Some consulates require Italian translations; others accept English. Confirm before spending money on professional translations.
  • Track the citizenship chain carefully. Ensure every ancestor from your Italian ancestor to you has documented proof of citizenship transfer. Missing a single certificate can derail the entire application.

Common issues in US applications

  • Naturalization events (Minor Issue): If an ancestor naturalized while their child was under 21, that child lost Italian citizenship. Citizenship Italia screens for this early.
  • 1948 cases: If your lineage passes through a female ancestor whose child was born before January 1, 1948, you may need a court petition instead of consulate application.
  • Missing records: Older naturalization records, vital records, or court documents may be lost or damaged. NARA and USCIS can provide Certificates of Non-Existence in such cases.
  • Name changes: Ancestors who changed names (anglicized surnames, spelling changes, etc.) can make records difficult to locate. Cross-reference maiden names, nicknames, and spelling variations.
  • Consulate document requests: During processing, consulates sometimes request follow-up documents or clarifications. This can add months to processing. Having excellent documentation prepared upfront minimizes requests.
