Apostille and Translation Requirements
for Italian Citizenship: Complete Guide

Learn which documents need apostille authentication, what must be translated into Italian, and how to avoid costly rejections.

Last updated: April 2026

Written by Citizenship Italia Legal Team · Citizenship Italia Last updated: April 2026

Key takeaways

  • An apostille is official authentication proving a document's origin is genuine
  • Vital records (birth, marriage, death) and court documents must be apostilled and translated into Italian
  • Naturalization records typically do not need translation; the consulate provides interpretation
  • Consulate translation requirements vary by location — always confirm with your specific office first
  • Court cases have stricter translation requirements; documents may need Italian-certified translators

What is an apostille?

An apostille is an official certification that authenticates the origin of a public document. It is a certification added by a government authority that confirms the signature, seal, stamp, and official character of a document are genuine and come from the stated source. The apostille is recognized internationally under the Hague Convention of 1961.

For example: You submit your birth certificate to the Massachusetts Registry of Vital Records requesting an apostille. The state authentication office examines the document, verifies the vital records seal, and affixes an apostille certification. The apostille states that the signature on the certificate is authentic and belongs to an official registrar of vital records. This apostille makes the document acceptable to Italian consulates and courts without requiring further authentication.

Italian consulates and courts require apostilles on almost all documents from North America because Italy is a signatory to the Hague Convention. The apostille serves as proof that the document is legitimate and was issued by a proper government authority.

Which documents require an apostille?

You must obtain apostilles for:

  • Vital records: All birth, marriage, and death certificates in your citizenship chain
  • Divorce decrees: If applicable in your lineage
  • Court judgments: Custody decrees, naturalization orders, or any court document relevant to your case
  • Official transcripts: If submitting educational records (rare for citizenship but sometimes required)
  • Power of attorney documents: If you are authorizing representation by an Italian attorney
  • Notarized documents: Affidavits, sworn statements, or notary-certified documents

Documents that do not require apostille:

  • Private correspondence or family records
  • Photographs or personal documents
  • Newspaper clippings or archival research

How to obtain an apostille in the United States

In the United States, the Secretary of State of the state that issued your document is responsible for issuing apostilles. Each state maintains a vital records office or archives office.

For vital records (birth, marriage, death certificates):

  • Contact the vital records office of the state where the event occurred (e.g., Massachusetts Registry of Vital Records for a Massachusetts birth)
  • Request certified copies with apostille authentication
  • Specify that the apostille must be in English and affixed to the document
  • Most states charge $5–$30 per apostille (in addition to the certified copy fee)
  • Processing takes 1–4 weeks by mail; expedited options are often available

For court documents:

  • Contact the clerk of the court that issued the document
  • Request a certified copy with apostille from the court seal office
  • Court apostilles typically take 2–6 weeks

What documents need to be translated into Italian?

For consulate applications, the following documents must be translated into Italian:

  • Birth certificates (all in the lineage)
  • Marriage certificates (all in the lineage)
  • Death certificates (all in the lineage, if the applicant is deceased)
  • Divorce decrees (if applicable)

Documents that typically do not require translation:

  • Naturalization records: The consulate has official interpreters and will read the document themselves
  • Power of attorney documents: These are often not translated; you provide the original in English
  • Divorce decrees from US courts: Consulates often accept these in English without translation

Important caveat: Translation requirements vary significantly between consulates. Some consulates are flexible and accept documents in English; others require Italian translation for almost everything. Before spending money on translations, contact your specific consulate and ask what language documents must be in.

Translation requirements for court cases

For court applications in Italy (such as ATQ or 1948 cases), translation requirements are stricter:

  • All vital records must be translated into Italian
  • Naturalization records and government documents must be translated into Italian
  • Translations must be certified by a professional translator recognized by an Italian court
  • Some courts require a translator to be physically present in Italy (not remote translation)

Your Italian attorney will typically arrange court-approved translations, as they have relationships with certified court translators in Rome or the applicable jurisdiction.

How to get a certified translation

For consulate applications:

  • Hire a professional translator certified by a recognized professional organization (such as the American Translators Association)
  • The translator should provide a statement certifying that the translation is accurate and complete
  • Some consulates require the translator to be a "certified translator" (meaning certified by the state or profession); confirm with your consulate
  • Cost: Typically $20–$50 USD per vital record certificate
  • Turnaround: 1–2 weeks for professional translators

For court applications:

  • Your Italian attorney will coordinate with certified court translators in Italy
  • These translators are officially registered with Italian courts and provide sworn translations
  • Cost: €50–€150 per document, plus administrative fees
  • Turnaround: 2–4 weeks after you provide the original documents

US-specific guidance: Vital records by state

Each US state maintains its own vital records office. Here are contact methods for requesting apostilles:

  • New York: New York Department of Health (VitalRecords.org portal) — apostille available, typically $15–$30
  • California: California Department of Public Health (vitalrecords.cdph.ca.gov) — apostille available, typically $15–$30
  • Illinois: Illinois Department of Public Health (dph.illinois.gov) — apostille available, typically $10–$25
  • Massachusetts: Massachusetts Registry of Vital Records (mass.gov) — apostille available, typically $20–$30
  • Pennsylvania: Pennsylvania Department of Health (pa.gov) — apostille available, typically $20–$35

For any state, search "[State Name] Vital Records Office" or contact the Secretary of State's office for guidance on requesting apostilles.

Common apostille and translation rejection reasons

The consulate may reject documents if:

  • Apostille is missing or incomplete: The apostille certification must be affixed directly to the document or as an attachment; a photocopy of an apostille is not accepted
  • Apostille is expired: Apostilles do not technically expire, but consulates may reject old apostilles (predating the Law 74/2025 rules) if they lack certain security features
  • Translation is not certified: The translator must be a professional and must provide a certification statement; machine translations or amateur translations are rejected outright
  • Translation omits translator credentials: The translator must include their name, professional credentials, and a sworn statement certifying accuracy
  • Translation is incomplete or contains errors: If the consulate officer identifies inconsistencies or gaps, the entire translation may be rejected
  • Original document is damaged or illegible: If the apostille or document itself is damaged, the consulate may reject it and request a new certified copy
  • Document format does not match consulate standards: Some consulates require vital records to be on official state forms; photocopies or altered documents are rejected

Best practices to avoid rejection

  • Confirm requirements with your consulate first. Call or email your specific consulate's citizenship office and ask exactly which documents need translation and what translation standard they require.
  • Order certified copies and apostilles together. When requesting vital records, request apostilles at the same time to avoid multiple order processing fees and delays.
  • Use professional, certified translators. Never attempt machine translation or use unqualified translators. The cost difference is minimal, and rejections are expensive in terms of time.
  • Request translations directly from the translator. Do not photocopy translations. Each translation should be provided as an original certified copy.
  • Keep originals safe and make extra copies. Order extra certified copies of vital records and apostilles — you may need duplicates for multiple applications or court cases.
  • Plan for delays. Build extra time into your application timeline for apostille and translation processing. If you need documents quickly, use expedited services and factor in the extra cost.
